Enclave

For a 10-week part-time UX Design course at General Assembly, I worked on an app of my own creation. The result is Enclave, a way for gender non-conforming (GNC) people to find and share their favorite places.

The Challenge

Gender non-conforming (GNC*) people need a way to find places to go without compromising their physical or psychological safety.

*The term GNC refers to anyone whose appearance, actions and/or gender identity don't match with cultural norms based on their biological sex.


The Approach

A mobile app which allows GNC people to find and share experiences of anyplace.


Goals

  • Identify a problem which is important and lacking a solution today.
  • Identify the target audience for the stated problem, and understand their wants, needs, priorities and pain points.
  • Design the MVP for a product to address the needs of the target audience, utilizing modern design and UX principles.

Duration

10 weeks


Tools

Pen, Paper, Index Cards, Post-It Notes, Sketch, InVision, Figma, OptimalWorkshop


My Role

User Researcher, UX Designer, UI Designer

Design Brief

There are many apps available today which allow anyone the ability to rate and review virtually anywhere. However, these apps are focused on sharing information which would apply to anyone generically; they don't support the particular interests and needs of more niche audiences, in this case GNC people. My project sought to address this challenge by proposing a way for GNC people to share the information most relevant to their needs.

Phase 1: User Research

Primary Research: Interviews

I started by interviewing a number of GNC friends and colleagues using the following research guide



Problem Statement

The problem I suspect is GNC people feel uncomfortable in public, gendered places.


Purpose

Understand in what places trans/non-binary/gender non-conforming people feel comfortable vs uncomfortable.


Research Outcome(s)

  • What public places to participants consider gendered?
  • What contributes to participants feeling comfortable vs uncomfortable?
  • How do participants navigate unfamiliar locations?

Method(s)

In-person and remote interviews


Measures

Location, Ethnicity, Gender Identity


Participants

Adults identifying as GNC, living in a major US city.



Going into the interviews, I had my own definition of gendered places (primarily bathrooms, locker rooms, dressing rooms and clothing stores/departments). I knew my definition was an assumption, and wanted to avoid simply leading my interviewees to validate those assumptions. Thus, I focused on learning which places made my interviewees comfortable vs un-comfortable without any limiting factors on the type. From that I hoped to get insight into a more encompassing definition of gendered places.


Research Insight: When you're a gender minority, the world is a gendered place.

My interviews revealed that I was incorrect with my internal definition of gendered places. Great! Interviews, like usability tests, are most interesting and valuable when they reveal that you would have gone in the wrong direction without them.


Secondary Research: Competitive Analysis

I also conducted a competitive analysis of several other services and apps available which allow for sharing information and/or social connections. I utilized a simple 2x2 to organize these competitors by demographic ("For Everyone" vs "GNC specific") and type of sharing ("Social Network" vs "Sharing/Reviews/Ratings"). This analysis was valuable in revealing that there really isn't a product available today geared at GNC people for sharing specific ratings or reviews.


User Personas

I started the process of creating a persona by organizing all interview data with affinity mapping.


From there, I came up with a primary user persona (Alex), which I used to guide the development of the app going forward. I also created an updated problem statement based on the persona.

Problem Statement v2

Alex needs a way to find places other GNC people frequent because those places are most likely to provide a feeling of psychological and physical safety.

Phase 2: Interaction Design

Design Strategy

Based on Alex's needs and the offerings in similar apps, I decided on the following list of priorities for the MVP.

First, emphasize finding and sharing good experiences over bad. Good experiences are more actionable than bad. If the world is an unknown place, with a base assumption that any place new could be un-comfortable, then sharing bad experiences doesn't answer the critical question "where should I go?". These will be important, but not a critical feature for the MVP.

Second, allow knowing personal details of other reviewers while maintaining anonymity. This one is a little bit of a "wicked problem". It's important for Alex to understand how much of their background other reviewers share, as that's a proxy for how well those folks experiences will map to Alex's. However, it's also important that Alex not have to provide personally identifiable information (PII) to the app as they're concerned about their privacy.

Third, make sharing the minimum amount of useful information in a review as frictionless as possible. To encourage Alex to give back to the community of other users, I need to define the smallest bit of useful information to share, and make that process as easy as possible.


Information Architecture part 1: User Flow

In line with the first priority listed above, I developed a primary user flow focused on Alex finding a place they want to go and getting there. This flow is relatively straight forward, but it was still important to understand the decision points Alex will be faced with. Mapping the flow also helps prevent designing a dead end, where users would end up stuck and frustrated.


Card Sorting

Based on the primary flow above, along with the other two MVP priorities, I created a list of ~30 proposed features. I then conducted an open card sort with several new participants. Performing an open card sort was important for revealing how people mentally classify the cards, and what specific terms they use. As I saw in the interview stage, limiting folks based on my own ideas for categories could be detrimental to the entire project.

You can see the entire list of proposed cards here.


Information Architecture part 2: Site Map

I used the groupings that emerged from the card sort to generate a site map proposing the initial information architecture for the app.


Wireframing & Prototyping

Time to start creating prototypes! I started with hand drawn, low-fidelity wireframes to play with ideas for some of the main screens. I took the versions I liked best, and translated those into digital wireframes using Sketch. I then used InVision to create interactive prototypes, in order to evaluate how people engaged with the proposed screens and flows. InVision was a great choice as it allows direct upload of screens from Sketch (with the Craft plugin), and is easy to learn, thus minimizing time needed to set up the prototype itself, and maximizing time available for testing.

Phase 3: Usability Testing & Iteration

The first round of testing was incredibly valuable. It revealed big opportunities for improving the key functionality for the app - sharing "favorites" (aka places where people had good experiences). I was using a 5 star rating system, similar to other apps like Yelp and Google. For Enclave, that ended up not being the best choice for the following reasons:

  • It's hard to quantify how comfortable someone feels. What does it mean to feel 5 stars vs 2 stars of comfortable or safe?
  • The app feels like a clone of the other rating apps, but with a few different categories. What's the real differentiator?

I utilized Google's design system, Material Design, with a customized 3 color scheme, to make the jump from grey-scale wireframes into mid-fidelity mockups. Another round of usability tests plus feedback from our instructor revealed some additional information:

  • Make on-boarding robust. That flow wasn't mapped out initially, but it's absence was a missed opportunity to reinforce who the target audience is.
  • Some people preferred viewing search results in a list and others the map. Having both those options was a good choice.
  • The entire friends concept was not important enough for the MVP. I initially thought that would be important for the sharing aspect, but that didn't turn out to be true.

Final Version

Taking into account the results of the usability tests, I iterated on the existing screens and added an onboarding flow.

Further Improvements

Test the untested. There were elements, like onboarding, that didn't go through a round of usability tests.

Niche experience. If this were a live project and/or the class had lasted a bit longer, at least one more round of iteration would help bring out the ways in which Enclave could really feel like a unique app for a GNC audience.

Professional polish. The final version of my app as of the end of the GA course is a mid-fidelity mockup. It's starting to look and feel real, but doesn't have the polish or visual sophistication of a professional mobile app in 2019. Given time constraints and that my professional background is in analytics and engineering rather than visual design, I had to prioritize working on functionality over UI enhancements.

Key Takeaways

Get frequent feedback from customers. I can't say for certain what Enclave would have looked like if I had proceeded on only my own instincts without user feedback. Not without inventing a time machine to explore that alternate path, which feels like both overkill and a waste of a time machine. I can say that every time I talked with users, I learned something that helped shift my strategy in ways I wouldn't have come up with solo.

Get feedback from others with more experience than you. One of the most valuable parts about doing the General Assembly course was the regular feedback we got from our instructor, a seasoned Design professional. In addition, the final product was evaluated by a group of other Design and UX pros with decades of experience collectively. All of that input was critical not just in shaping the development of Enclave, but in identifying my own UX strengths and weaknesses to be mindful of as I continue on this path.
